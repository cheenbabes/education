- **Order and Sequence**: Activities are presented in logical sequences, allowing children to build skills progressively and understand the order in their environment.

- **Spontaneous Creative Expression**: Children are encouraged to express themselves creatively through music, drama, and crafts, with ample materials provided for exploration.
- **Freedom of Choice**: Children should have the freedom to choose their activities and explore their environment, enabling them to develop their unseen capabilities.
- **Sensory-Based Learning**: Learning should engage multiple senses (visual, auditory, vestibular, tactile) to maximize understanding and skill acquisition.
- **Minimal Adult Intervention**: Adults should guide only when necessary, allowing children to direct their own learning and develop independence.
- **Prepared Environment**: Teachers are responsible for preparing a motivating and structured environment that encourages exploration and learning.
- **Respect for the Child**: Each learner is a unique individual deserving respect, autonomy, and individual attention.
- **Holistic Development**: Education should address the whole child, including intellectual, physical, and emotional growth.

- **Parental and Teacher Collaboration**: Effective learning involves cooperation and communication between teachers, parents, and children.
- **Self-Paced Progress**: Children should be allowed to progress at their own pace, choosing materials and activities suited to their interests and developmental stage.

- **Self-Directed Activity**: Children learn best through spontaneous, self-chosen activities that respond to their internal needs and interests.

- **Control of Error**: Materials and activities should be designed so that children can recognize and correct their own mistakes, fostering independence and self-correction.
- **Joy in Work**: True learning and development occur when children find joy and satisfaction in meaningful work, which prevents fatigue and supports well-being.
- **Development of Will and Attention**: The will and attention are developed through repeated, focused activity chosen by the child, not by subjection or forced discipline.
- **Moral and Social Development**: Moral education arises from real experiences, mutual aid, and satisfaction of intellectual needs, not from imposed stories or rewards and punishments.

- **Scientific Observation**: Teachers should observe children closely to understand their needs and interests, using these observations to guide and support learning.

- **Social and Moral Development**: Education should foster social cooperation, patience, and moral values through meaningful participation in community life.

### Principles (37)

- **Frequent Nature Exposure**: Children benefit from regular and early opportunities to play and learn outdoors in plant-rich, natural settings.
- **Physical Activity through Nature**: Outdoor play in natural environments encourages movement and physical development, helping to reduce sedentary behavior and associated health risks.
- **Fostering Naturalistic Intelligence**: Learning about local plants, animals, and ecosystems develops children's naturalistic intelligence and environmental stewardship.
- **Child-Led Exploration**: Children should be respected as competent learners and risk-takers, directing their own play and investigations in nature.
- **Scientific Inquiry in Nature**: Children’s curiosity about the natural world can be harnessed to develop observation, documentation, and investigation skills.
- **Social and Emotional Growth**: Nature play supports social skills, imagination, stress relief, and a sense of belonging and responsibility toward living things.
- **Integrating Indoors and Outdoors**: Activities and materials can move fluidly between indoor and outdoor environments, enriching learning in both contexts.
- **Teacher Engagement and Modeling**: Teachers play a crucial role by modeling curiosity, providing support, and ensuring safety during outdoor learning experiences.
- **Learning in Nature**: Education should occur in the context of nature, with learning taking place outdoors where nature acts as a 'third teacher' and the environment is central to the curriculum.
- **Student-Centered Discovery**: Nature-based education emphasizes student autonomy, allowing learners to explore, discover, and learn through direct experience rather than passive instruction.
- **Holistic Skill Development**: Nature-based education supports academic, social-emotional, and life skills such as problem-solving, resilience, curiosity, compassion, and respect.
- **Integration Across Subjects**: Nature can be incorporated into all subjects, including math, science, art, and language, by connecting curriculum standards to real-world, nature-based experiences.
- **Inclusivity and Differentiation**: Recognizes that students learn differently and provides varied, hands-on opportunities to meet diverse needs, especially benefiting those who struggle in traditional settings.
- **Connection and Stewardship**: Fosters a sense of connection to the environment, encouraging care for nature and awareness of ecological issues.
- **Nature as Central Organizing Concept**: Nature is the integrating thread that intentionally ties together the program’s philosophy, methodologies, classroom design, outdoor spaces, and public identity.
- **Dual Focus on Child and Conservation**: Programs support both child development across all domains and the development of an ecological identity or environmental ethic.
- **Developmentally Appropriate and Environmental Education**: Teaching practices are grounded in both high-quality early childhood education and environmental education, requiring staff skilled in both areas.
- **Hands-on, Play-based Learning**: Learning is play-based and emphasizes hands-on, exploratory experiences, especially in natural settings.
- **Open-ended Materials Foster Creativity**: Children are provided with unstructured, open-ended materials from nature to encourage creativity, problem-solving, and teamwork.
- **Emphasis on Social-Emotional Development**: Programs intentionally support behavioral and emotional self-regulation, social skills, and positive peer interactions as foundational for later academic success.
- **Connection to Nature**: Increasing children’s connection with nature is seen as beneficial for physical, social, psychological, and academic development.
- **Nature as Essential Resource**: Natural outdoor environments are vital for advancing all domains of child development, health, and wellbeing, and should be intentionally integrated into early childhood education.
- **Diverse Natural Features**: Outdoor learning environments should include a variety of natural elements—such as plants, habitats, topographic variations, and natural materials—to enrich play, learning, and physical activity.
- **Structured and Unstructured Play**: Both structured and unstructured activities in outdoor settings are important for fostering physical, cognitive, and social-emotional growth.
- **Connection to Place and Community**: Learning outdoors should help children build understanding and appreciation of local ecosystems, food systems, and environmental processes, fostering a sense of stewardship.
- **Integration with Indoor Learning**: Outdoor activities should reinforce and connect with indoor learning experiences, creating a holistic educational approach.
- **Accessibility and Inclusion**: All children, including those with special needs, should have equitable access to quality outdoor environments that support diverse ways of playing and learning.
- **Daily Outdoor Time**: Children should spend a minimum of 30 minutes outdoors for every three hours of programming, weather permitting, to maximize developmental benefits.
- **Hands-on Environmental Stewardship**: Children should be given opportunities to care for living things and participate in activities that promote environmental responsibility.
- **Predictable Spaces**: Children thrive creatively when they have access to familiar, well-defined outdoor environments where materials and activity areas are consistently available.
- **Ample and Consistent Time**: Extended, uninterrupted blocks of time are essential for deep engagement, allowing children to immerse themselves in creative play and problem-solving.
- **Open-Ended Materials**: Providing a wide variety of natural, open-ended materials supports divergent thinking, imagination, and creative problem-solving.
- **Caring, Observant Adults**: Adults should observe closely, scaffold learning when needed, and respect children's autonomy, stepping in only to support or extend play without taking over.
- **Child-Led Exploration**: Children are encouraged to follow their interests and initiate their own activities, with adults facilitating rather than directing the learning process.
- **Flexible Use of Space and Materials**: Environments and materials should be adaptable, allowing children to move items and expand their play as needed for their creative processes.
- **Integration of Multiple Domains**: Creative play in nature supports development across physical, cognitive, social-emotional, and language domains simultaneously.
- **Connection to Nature**: Regular engagement with natural materials and outdoor environments fosters care for the earth and an appreciation for natural resources.

### Principles (51)

- **Driving Questions Motivate Learning**: PBL units are organized around a central, well-designed driving question that is feasible, worthwhile, contextualized, meaningful, and ethical, guiding and motivating student inquiry throughout the project.
- **Focus on Significant Learning Goals**: PBL should target important content standards, concepts, and in-depth understandings fundamental to academic disciplines, while also developing critical thinking, self-regulation, and collaboration skills.
- **Projects as Central Vehicle**: Projects are not an add-on but the main process through which learning occurs, serving as the 'spine' of the curriculum rather than a culminating activity.
- **Extended Inquiry and Time**: PBL requires sustained, in-depth investigations over an extended period, rather than brief or isolated project units.
- **Construct Knowledge Actively**: Students are active participants, constructing knowledge through inquiry, problem-solving, and critical thinking, often by creating artifacts or tangible products.
- **Cultivate Student Engagement**: Teachers foster a 'need to know' by engaging students with authentic roles and contexts, increasing motivation and readiness to learn.
- **Use Scaffolds and Support**: Teachers, peers, materials, and technology provide scaffolds tailored to students' current understanding, which are gradually removed as students gain independence.
- **Encourage Student Choice**: Students are given autonomy and input over aspects of their learning, such as team roles, tasks, questions, resources, and final products.
- **Support Collaborative Learning**: Collaboration is integral, with students working together throughout all project stages, mirroring real-world problem-solving.
- **Authentic Assessment and Reflection**: Assessment involves creating tangible products that answer the driving question, opportunities for student reflection and feedback, and presenting work to authentic public audiences.
- **Real-World Relevance**: Projects are designed to address real-world problems and issues, making learning meaningful and applicable.
- **Student Choice**: Students are encouraged to select topics based on their interests or career goals, fostering engagement and ownership.
- **Skill Integration**: Learning activities require students to use diverse skills such as researching, writing, interviewing, and collaborating.
- **Ongoing Reflection**: Evaluation and learning occur throughout the project via continuous reflection and revision.
- **Authentic Assessment**: Students demonstrate learning through the creation of various products, such as research papers, presentations, or performances.
- **Learning by Doing**: Students construct knowledge meaningfully through active investigation, exploration, and hands-on experiences rather than passive reception.
- **Authentic, Real-World Inquiry**: Learning is anchored in real-world problems and essential questions that are relevant to students' lives, fostering deeper engagement and understanding.
- **Student-Centered Approach**: Students take responsibility for their learning, making choices, collaborating, and driving the direction of projects with teacher guidance.
- **Collaboration and Communication**: Group work, teamwork, and effective communication are central, helping students develop social and cross-cultural skills.
- **Facilitator Role of Teacher**: Teachers act as facilitators and coaches, guiding inquiry and supporting students rather than delivering content directly.
- **Integration of 21st Century Skills**: PBL intentionally develops critical thinking, problem solving, creativity, self-management, and technology literacy.
- **Constructivist Foundation**: Learning builds on students' prior knowledge and experiences, enabling them to synthesize new understanding.
- **Backward Design for Curriculum**: Curriculum and projects are designed by identifying desired outcomes and assessment evidence before planning activities.
- **Reflection and Revision**: Students and teachers engage in ongoing reflection, self-assessment, and revision to improve understanding and project quality.
- **Authentic Assessment**: Assessment is based on real products and performances, using rubrics and feedback from teachers, peers, and sometimes public audiences.
- **Student-Centered Learning**: PBL shifts the teacher's role from delivering instruction to designing experiences and facilitating understanding, allowing students to take the lead in their learning.
- **Inquiry and Exploration**: Students engage in inquiry, exploring meaningful questions and problems throughout the project process.
- **Deeper Learning**: PBL is designed to promote deeper understanding of academic content, not just surface-level knowledge.
- **Reflection and Iteration**: Teachers and students reflect on lessons learned, assess what worked and what didn’t, and use feedback to improve future projects.
- **Collaboration and Community**: PBL encourages collaboration among students, teachers, and the broader educational community, fostering peer feedback and shared learning.
- **Real-World Relevance**: Projects are designed to help students find meaning in their work and connect learning to real-life contexts, preparing them for college, career, and life.
- **Continuous Professional Growth**: Teachers are encouraged to continually develop their PBL practice through workshops, peer groups, and reflection.
- **Student-Centered Learning**: Projects are student-driven, allowing learners to choose topics relevant to their interests and actively participate in planning, implementation, and evaluation.
- **Real-World Relevance**: Projects address real-world problems and result in tangible products or outcomes that have value beyond the classroom.
- **Interdisciplinary Approach**: Project-based instruction integrates multiple subject areas, helping students make connections across disciplines.
- **Active, Hands-On Engagement**: Learning is grounded in firsthand investigation, experimentation, and creation, rather than rote memorization.
- **Collaboration and Communication**: Students work together, share ideas, and develop teamwork and communication skills through group projects.
- **Reflective Thinking and Self-Assessment**: Students regularly reflect on their learning process, assess their progress, and set goals for improvement.
- **Teacher as Facilitator and Coach**: Teachers guide, support, and coach students through the project process, helping them set goals, access resources, and develop skills.
- **Authentic Assessment**: Assessment focuses on both the process and the final product, using real-world tasks, portfolios, presentations, and self-assessment.
- **Cultural Relevance and Inclusivity**: Projects are sensitive to local culture and allow students from diverse backgrounds to use their individual strengths and cultural perspectives.
- **Authentic Learning**: Projects are grounded in real-world problems and contexts, engaging students in meaningful inquiry and application beyond the classroom.
- **Student Choice and Voice**: Students have input in selecting project topics, shaping inquiry, and making decisions, fostering ownership and motivation.
- **Active Exploration**: Learning is hands-on and inquiry-driven, requiring students to investigate, experiment, and interact with materials, people, and environments.
- **Applied Learning**: Students apply knowledge and skills to create products, solve problems, and perform tasks that have value outside of school.

### Principles (58)

- **Threefold Human Being**: Human development is based on the interplay of thinking, feeling, and willing, each forming a core aspect of the soul and developing in distinct phases.
- **Developmental Phases**: Childhood is divided into three 7-year phases—early childhood (will), middle childhood (feeling), and adolescence (thinking)—each requiring different educational approaches.
- **Imitation in Early Childhood**: Young children learn primarily through imitation, unconsciously absorbing behaviors, attitudes, and habits from their environment and caregivers.
- **Learning Through Feeling**: In middle childhood, children learn best through experiences that engage their feelings, such as stories, art, and rhythm, rather than abstract instruction.
- **Individuality and Destiny**: Each child is seen as a unique individuality with a spiritual purpose, shaped by heredity, environment, and their own will, and education should support the unfolding of their destiny.
- **Environment Shapes Development**: The environment, including relationships and surroundings, deeply influences the child’s physical, emotional, and social development, especially in the early years.
- **Active, Play-Based Learning**: Children, especially in early childhood, need freedom to move, play, and explore, as physical activity is foundational for later cognitive and emotional growth.
- **Goodness, Beauty, Truth**: Each developmental phase emphasizes a core value: goodness in early childhood, beauty in middle childhood, and truth in adolescence.
- **Holistic Human Development**: Education addresses the physical, emotional, and spiritual development of each child, recognizing every person as a unique spiritual individuality progressing through developmental stages.
- **Developmentally Appropriate Curriculum**: Curriculum and teaching methods are tailored to distinct phases of child development, emphasizing imitation and play in early childhood, feeling and imagination in the grades, and thinking in adolescence.
- **Cultivation of Imagination and Creativity**: Imagination, inspiration, and intuition are consciously cultivated through artistic activities, storytelling, and creative play, forming the basis for later creative and academic work.
- **Human-Centered Relationships**: Education is built on strong, nurturing human relationships, with teachers modeling healthy social interactions and fostering a sense of community among students and families.
- **Rhythm and Repetition**: Daily, weekly, and seasonal rhythms, along with repetition in activities and stories, provide security, structure, and support for learning and healthy development.
- **Learning Through Doing**: Children learn best through direct experience, purposeful work, and hands-on activities, especially in early childhood, rather than abstract instruction.
- **Teacher Freedom and Artistic Presentation**: Teachers are expected to adapt and invent their pedagogical approach moment by moment, presenting material artistically and responsively to the needs of their students.
- **Threefold Social Order**: School governance and community life reflect the principles of freedom (cultural), equality (rights), and brotherhood (economic), fostering collaboration and mutual support.
- **Self-Education Environment**: Education is fundamentally self-education; adults provide the environment and conditions for children to educate themselves according to their own destinies.
- **Imitation and Example**: Children learn primarily through imitation of adults' gestures, attitudes, and meaningful activities, rather than through direct instruction.
- **Love and Warmth**: A loving and warm atmosphere is foundational for healthy development and is expressed in relationships among children, adults, and the broader community.
- **Care for Environment and Senses**: The physical and sensory environment, both indoors and outdoors, should be nourishing, orderly, and meaningful, supporting the child's active self-education.
- **Creative, Artistic Experience**: Artistic and creative experiences are essential, including music, movement, storytelling, and visual arts, integrated into daily life and rhythms.
- **Meaningful Adult Activity**: Adults engage in real, purposeful work that is visible to children, providing opportunities for imitation and fostering a sense of purpose and creativity.
- **Free, Imaginative Play**: Children need ample time and space for self-directed, imaginative play, with adults providing supportive conditions and gentle guidance.
- **Protection of Childhood Forces**: Children should be allowed to remain in a dreamlike, imaginative state, protected from premature intellectualization and excessive verbal instruction.
- **Gratitude, Reverence, and Wonder**: An atmosphere of gratitude, reverence, and wonder is cultivated through adult example, fostering love, devotion, and a positive relationship to the world.
- **Joy, Humor, and Happiness**: Joy, humor, and happiness are vital forces in the environment, shaping healthy development and relationships.
- **Adult Inner Development**: Educators are committed to ongoing self-education, inner development, and self-knowledge, recognizing their being as the most profound influence on the child.
- **Balance and Context Sensitivity**: Practices should be adapted to the needs and context of the children and environment, avoiding dogmatism or arbitrary personal preference.
- **Educating the Whole Child**: Education should meet the needs of the whole human being—body, soul, and spirit—addressing physical, emotional, and spiritual development.
- **Developmental Stages**: Child development is viewed in seven-year stages, with early childhood (birth to seven) focused on nurturing the physical body and learning through imitation.
- **Imitation and Example**: Young children learn primarily through imitation of adults and older children, making the educator's actions and environment central to learning.
- **Home-like Environment**: Learning takes place in a calm, beautiful, and sensorially protective environment that resembles a home, supporting emotional security and authentic learning.
- **Self-Development of the Educator**: Educators are expected to engage in continuous self-development to be worthy role models for children in thought, word, and deed.
- **Play as Central**: Self-initiated play is at the heart of early childhood education, seen as the child's way of exploring and developing their own learning needs.
- **Rhythm and Repetition**: Daily, weekly, and yearly rhythms and repetition in activities provide security, build confidence, and support learning.
- **Practical Life Skills**: Practical domestic and artistic activities are integral, helping children develop life skills and creativity through meaningful work.
- **Observation and Assessment**: Observation of the child is a key practice, with assessment being formative and based on understanding each child's unique development.
- **Partnership with Families**: Close partnership with parents and carers is essential, recognizing them as the child's first and primary educators.
- **Mixed-Age Grouping**: Children are grouped in mixed-age settings, allowing younger children to learn from older peers and fostering a familial atmosphere.
- **Spiritual View of the Child**: Each child is seen as a spiritual being with unique gifts and a destiny, deserving reverence and respect from educators.
- **Educate the Whole Person**: Education should address the intellectual, social, and individual development of each student, fostering qualification, socialisation, and individuation.
- **Developmentally Appropriate Practice**: Curriculum and pedagogy are structured to align with distinct developmental stages, with educational approaches tailored to the needs of each age group.
- **Emergent and Contextual Curriculum**: Curriculum is not fixed but evolves in response to local culture, social needs, and individual student development, guided by generative principles.
- **Learning as Transformation**: Learning is seen as a transformative, lifelong process that develops potentialities into enhanced capacities through meaningful engagement with the world.
- **Learning Community**: Learning occurs within communities of practice, where participation, dialogue, and relationships are central to development.
- **Teacher as Role Model**: Teachers are seen as role models whose values, attitudes, and relationships with students are decisive for learning.
- **Holistic Assessment**: Assessment is ongoing, formative, and reflective, supporting both learner development and educational excellence rather than focusing solely on outcomes.
- **Inclusivity and Care**: Schools strive to be inclusive, respectful of difference, and foster an ethic of care, tolerance, and peaceful conflict resolution.
- **Rhythm and Balance**: Learning and teaching are structured rhythmically, balancing explicit instruction with opportunities for self-directed and participatory learning.
- **Educate Head, Heart, Hands**: Education should address intellectual, emotional, and practical aspects of the child, with each developmental stage emphasizing a different aspect.
- **Teach Through Imagination**: Imagination is the key to reaching children in the early grades; lessons should be presented through stories, pictures, and concrete imagery rather than abstract explanation.
- **Developmentally Appropriate Timing**: Academic content is introduced only when the child shows readiness, focusing on physical and emotional development before intellectual work.
- **Holistic, Multidisciplinary Learning**: Subjects are integrated into main lesson blocks, connecting academic, artistic, and practical activities for deeper understanding.
- **Rhythm and Habit**: A consistent daily and lesson rhythm, along with the cultivation of positive habits, supports healthy development and learning.
- **Learning by Doing**: Hands-on, practical experiences are essential for learning, especially when combining strong and weak areas of intelligence.
- **Teacher as Guide and Model**: The parent or teacher is the main provider of knowledge, modeling attitudes and behaviors, and preparing thoroughly for each lesson.
- **Memory Before Understanding**: Children memorize verses, poems, and stories, holding them in their minds to be understood more deeply in later years.
- **Protect Childhood**: Children should not be rushed into intellectual or individualized activities; their development is protected by appropriate content and group experiences.
