## 2. Market Buckets (Updated)

Since the primary analysis, the market has not materially changed structure. It still sorts into four buckets:

### Bucket 1 — ChatGPT Wrappers ($5–12/mo)
MagicSchool, Chalkie, Eduaide, LessonPlanGen, PlanSpark, Almanack, Brisk, Edcafe. These are AI tools that generate generic lesson plans. They market to teachers, not homeschool parents. The lesson quality reflects what you'd get from a well-prompted ChatGPT query. No philosophy implementation, no archetype, no curriculum matching. They have scale (MagicSchool has the largest user base in this category), but they're not competing for the same customer.

### Bucket 2 — Philosophy Namedroppers ($16–39/mo)
LearnSpark ($39), LittleLit ($16.50), Kuraplan (freemium). These mention Montessori or Charlotte Mason in their marketing. None implement it at the level we do. LearnSpark says "adaptable to Montessori, PBL" — that's a checkbox. Kuraplan supports 4 philosophies but without a knowledge graph or primary-source grounding. **This is our primary competitive segment.** We are materially better than all of them at the specific thing they're trying to do, and we're 54% cheaper than the most prominent one (LearnSpark at $39).

### Bucket 3 — Traditional Curriculum Packages ($300–1,000/year/child)
Sonlight, Simply Charlotte Mason, Multisori, Waldorfish, The Good and the Beautiful. Real, trusted, philosophy-specific — but siloed to one philosophy, no AI, no adaptability, no standards tracking. The parents who currently buy these are our primary acquisition target. They're philosophy-committed, they've already proven willingness to spend, and they're doing manually what we automate.

### Bucket 4 — Institutional Platforms ($30–40+/mo per student)
Time4Learning, Schoolio, Math Academy. Per-student pricing, accreditation focus. Not our customer, and their per-family cost for 2+ children dwarfs ours ($79.90–$119.96/month vs. our $17.99 flat).

**We occupy a fifth category with no current occupant: philosophy-first homeschool AI platform.**

### The Value Comparison That Matters

Homeschool families don't mentally compare us to other apps — they compare us to their existing curriculum spend:

| What families currently spend | Annual cost |
|---|---|
| Single all-in-one curriculum package | $300–$1,000/child |
| Individual subject curricula (2–3 subjects) | $150–$600/child |
| Online curriculum subscription | $300–$800/child |
| **Our Homestead (family flat-rate)** | **$215.88/year (all children)** |

For a family with 2–3 kids using a traditional curriculum, we cost less than one subject package for one child. The value proposition is overwhelming once framed this way.

### Will People Pay?

Yes. Three reasons:

1. **The Compass Quiz creates pre-purchase investment.** Parents who complete the quiz and receive their archetype result have had a genuine "that's exactly me" moment. That psychological recognition is the most powerful precondition for conversion. The free tier is not just a trial — it's the product's best sales tool.

2. **The target customer has already proven willingness to spend.** Parents who care about Charlotte Mason, Montessori, and Classical education are not price-sensitive in the way commodity homeschool content buyers are. They've bought $80 living books. They pay $700/year for Sonlight. They invest in curriculum because they believe in it. $17.99/month is a low ask.

3. **The free tier is correctly scoped.** 3 lessons/month is enough to prove quality — not enough to run a full school year for free. Top curriculum matches show value without unlocking the full matching engine. The Compass Quiz has no paid gate. This is the correct balance.

### One Pricing Note

The original analysis recommended $14.99/month. The live price is $17.99. The difference is small but worth noting: $14.99 sits clearly "under $15" in the shopper's mental model; $17.99 sits in the same zone as $20. Neither is wrong. If conversion data shows friction at the upgrade step, a promotional $14.99/month annual-commitment offer would be the first thing to test — not a permanent price reduction.

**Annual pricing is missing from the live pricing section.** This is the most important closeable gap in the pricing structure. Ed-tech churn runs ~9.6%/month on monthly plans. Annual plans cut that dramatically. Displaying an annual option ("$179/year — save $36") on the pricing section is a standard practice that every competitor in our range uses (MagicSchool, LessonPlanGen, Chalkie). It should be added.

## 4. Closeable Gaps

These are the areas where competitors have something we don't. All are closeable with relatively low effort.

### Gap 1: Annual Pricing Option
**Competitive coverage:** MagicSchool, LessonPlanGen, Chalkie, LittleLit, Almanack, PlanSpark — essentially every competitor at our price point.
**What it requires:** Add annual billing option to Stripe/Clerk, display the annual/monthly toggle on the pricing section.
**Impact:** Reduces monthly churn, improves LTV, gives cost-conscious families a lower barrier.

### Gap 2: Worksheet / Printable Generation
**Competitive coverage:** MagicSchool, Chalkie, Eduaide, LessonPlanGen.
**What it requires:** Add a "Generate worksheet" button on completed lessons. The lesson content is already structured — worksheet generation is a thin layer on top.
**Impact:** Addresses a high-frequency homeschool parent need. Worksheets are not our core value prop but they're a "why not?" feature that removes an objection.

### Gap 3: Explicit Neurodivergent/2E Marketing
**Competitive coverage:** LittleLit ($16.50) has made this a primary differentiator. MagicSchool has IEP/504 tools.
**What it requires:** No product change needed — the Compass Quiz already captures learning needs, and multi-child differentiation already handles per-child customization. This is a messaging gap, not a product gap. Add "neurodivergent-friendly" and "2E families" language to the homepage and a dedicated section or FAQ.
**Impact:** Homeschool families with 2E children are a large, vocal, underserved segment who are actively searching for philosophy-aligned + accommodating solutions. LittleLit's ADHD/autism angle has made them sticky with this group despite a weaker product overall.

### Gap 4: Worldschool / Location-Flexibility Messaging
**Competitive coverage:** LearnSpark markets offline access and worldschooling explicitly.
**What it requires:** A messaging addition, not a product change. Our lessons don't depend on a physical location; they're designed to be interest-driven and nature-based options are already part of the 8 philosophies. A small "works anywhere" or "worldschooling-friendly" note in the feature section or audience tabs would claim this segment.
**Impact:** Worldschooling families are a smaller but high-willingness-to-pay segment who feel underserved by US-centric tools.

## 5. Positioning Summary

**What we are:** The only platform that starts with *who you are as a teacher*, matches you to the right curriculum, and then generates lessons that actually implement your philosophy — not just label it.

**The one-sentence difference:** Every other tool generates lessons. We generate lessons *for you* — your philosophy, your children, your state's standards, your interests.

**Primary acquisition target:** Philosophy-committed homeschool parents currently spending $300–$1,000/year on traditional curriculum packages. These are the families using Simply Charlotte Mason, Sonlight, Classical Conversations co-ops, or Montessori kits. They have proven spend, strong philosophy identity, and the highest probability of recognizing our value immediately.

**The conversion thesis:** The Compass Quiz is the product. If the archetype result lands — if the parent reads "The Cultivator" and thinks *that's me* — the sale is nearly done. Optimizing the quiz result page for that recognition moment is the highest-leverage conversion work available.
